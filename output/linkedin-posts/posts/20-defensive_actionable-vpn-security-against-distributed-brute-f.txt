Distributed brute-force systems are targeting 8 specific VPN protocols with enterprise-grade automation.

Here's how to defend against $50,000 attack infrastructure:

→ PRIORITY PROTOCOLS TO SECURE
Fortinet SSL-VPN, Cisco AnyConnect, SonicWall SSL-VPN, WatchGuard, GlobalProtect (Palo Alto), Sophos, RDWeb, Citrix. These dominate both legitimate deployments and automated attack tooling.

→ IMPLEMENT ACCOUNT LOCKOUT + RATE LIMITING
Configure aggressive lockout policies on all internet-facing VPN endpoints. Distributed systems rotate proxies but target the same accounts — lockouts break their automation.

→ MONITOR FOR PROXY-ROTATED PATTERNS
Watch authentication logs for: multiple failed attempts across different accounts from rotating source IPs, low-and-slow brute-force patterns designed to avoid detection, geographic inconsistencies in login attempts.

→ AUDIT MFA ENFORCEMENT
Scan for default, test, service, or migrated accounts lacking MFA. These are primary targets for automated credential attacks. Every non-MFA account is an automation target.

→ RESTRICT MANAGEMENT INTERFACES
Limit admin interfaces to trusted IP ranges. Reduces externally reachable attack surface for network scanning tools like Masscan.

→ IMPLEMENT BEHAVIORAL MONITORING
Track normal vs. abnormal authentication patterns. Distributed tooling creates statistical anomalies even with proxy rotation.

The key insight: these systems are sophisticated but predictable. Consistent defensive measures break their automation.
