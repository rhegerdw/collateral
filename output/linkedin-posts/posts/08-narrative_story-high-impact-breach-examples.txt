The numbers tell one story. The individual cases tell another. Jaguar Land Rover: $2.5 billion in estimated losses. Scattered Spider's Marks & Spencer breach: prolonged operational disruption and significant stock depreciation. Interlock's DaVita attack: 2.7 million patient records exposed.

These weren't just statistics in the 28% of victims who refused to pay. They were organizations that gambled on containment and lost to secondary monetization.

The Dark Angels group's $75 million payday proves the other side: when critical infrastructure or mission-essential operations are at stake, the calculus changes completely.

Both outcomes — devastating losses from refusal and record payments for compliance — illustrate why the binary "pay or don't pay" framework no longer captures the real decision tree.
