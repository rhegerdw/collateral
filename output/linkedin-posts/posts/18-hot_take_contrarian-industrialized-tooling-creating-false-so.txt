$50,000 buys you enterprise-grade offensive infrastructure. But that doesn't make you a sophisticated threat actor.

We're confusing tooling capability with operator skill.

Distributed brute-force systems with web dashboards, proxy rotation, fault tolerance — this looks like nation-state infrastructure. But it's commercially available software with lifetime technical support.

Traditional threat modeling assumes advanced capabilities require advanced skills. APT-level tooling meant APT-level operators.

Not anymore.

When sophisticated attacks are productized, the barrier drops from skill development to purchasing power. Anyone can deploy enterprise-grade offensive capabilities.

But tooling sophistication doesn't equal operational sophistication. Automated brute-forcing is still just credential stuffing at scale.

We're seeing capability inflation without skill elevation. The tooling evolved faster than the operators.

This creates attribution challenges: sophisticated techniques from unsophisticated actors. The correlation between capability and skill level has broken.

Stop assuming tool sophistication indicates threat actor sophistication.
