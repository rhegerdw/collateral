Leak Bazaar: Emergence of Post-Exfiltration Data Monetization Infrastructure
BLOT: Since October 2025, Darkweb IQ operatives have tracked the development of Leak Bazaar, a criminal marketplace designed to convert exfiltrated corporate data from non-paying ransomware victims into structured, sellable intelligence products. DWIQ first identified the platform through direct engagement with SnowTeam, a known initial access broker (IAB) with assessed ties to Akira ransomware. The earliest known public reporting on Leak Bazaar appeared on March 25, 2026.
The Monetization Gap
The ransomware ecosystem’s economics shifted substantially in 2025. According to Chainalysis’s annual cryptocurrency crime report, published in February 2026, the share of ransomware victims who paid a ransom fell to 28% in 2025, down from approximately 63% in 2024 and 79% in 2022. Total on-chain ransomware payments declined to approximately $820 million in 2025. At the same time, the number of claimed attacks rose 50% year over year, marking 2025 as the most active year on record, with more than 8,000 organizations publicly named on leak sites.
DWIQ operational data, derived from sustained direct engagement with initial access brokers, indicates that the average price for network access declined from $1,427 in Q1 2023 to $439 in Q1 2026, a deflation likely driven by automation, AI-assisted tooling, and oversupply from infostealer logs. While the average price of bulk access has declined, premium access types, such as domain administrator credentials, Citrix environments with full internal network reach, and cloud management consoles, continue to command prices ranging from several thousand to tens of thousands of dollars.
Despite the decline in overall payment rates, high-impact outliers persist. The 2025 attack on Jaguar Land Rover caused estimated losses of approximately $2.5 billion. The Scattered Spider group’s breach of Marks & Spencer produced prolonged operational disruption and significant stock depreciation. Interlock’s attack on DaVita exposed 2.7 million patient records. The single largest known ransom payment in recent history, $75 million to the Dark Angels group, demonstrates that while fewer victims are paying, those who do are often paying substantially more, with the median ransom payment rising 368% year over year.
The combined effect of these shifts e.g. rising attack volume, declining payment rates, deflating access prices, and an oversupply of compromised credentials, has resulted in a growing inventory of exfiltrated corporate data that operators are unable to monetize through traditional extortion. Leak Bazaar appears designed to address precisely this gap. In addition to a ransom-centric model, these developments indicate an attempt at a broader data monetization economy.
DWIQ Collection Timeline
SnowTeam, the actor advertising Leak Bazaar, is a long-standing unwitting criminal source for DWIQ. DWIQ previously assessed SnowTeam as an IAB with ties to Akira ransomware, as reported in the October 2025 Monthly Report, which first described a marketplace designed to monetize exfiltrated data rather than rely solely on conventional name-and-shame tactics. At that time, several victims advertised on the site corresponded to organizations previously listed as Akira victims, and the broker confirmed the affiliation to a DWIQ undercover operative.
In late March 2026, SnowTeam publicly advertised Leak Bazaar on the Exploit cybercriminal forum. The thread was subsequently deleted, and SnowTeam was banned from Exploit for advertising ransomware-related products. SnowTeam then complained on a different forum that ReHub, described in underground circles as the most successful XSS clone, had also removed advertisements for both Leak Bazaar and the brute-force tool. SnowTeam expressed concern about the pattern of removals.
Following the Exploit ban, SnowTeam migrated to T1erOne, a forum DWIQ operatives assess resembles a rebooted RAMP. DWIQ operatives maintain a presence on T1erOne and confirmed SnowTeam’s advertisements there. The Leak Bazaar advertisement was posted on March 25, 2026, and SnowTeam is running a 10% referral commission program for new data suppliers. SnowTeam also indicated to DWIQ operatives that a video demonstrating the data analysis software would be released to prospective clients, stating in Russian that even a demonstration of an older version would be sufficient to prove the platform’s legitimacy.
T1erOne is notable for more than SnowTeam’s presence. DWIQ operatives observed that Qilin, Medusa, Hyflock, Gunra, and a group calling itself “The Gentlemen’s RaaS” are also advertising on the forum. The forum maintains a public blacklist of RaaS affiliates who violate platform rules. At the time of observation, three affiliates had been blacklisted: two Nova affiliates and one Qilin affiliate. One of the blacklisted Nova affiliates was identified as a researcher. SnowTeam has also confirmed through direct engagement that any and all ransomware groups are welcome to supply data to Leak Bazaar, marking a transition from a single-group monetization tool to a multi-supplier exchange.
The sequence of events, advertisements removed from top-tier forums, migration to a newer forum with active RaaS group presence, and the introduction of a referral program, illustrates a recurring pattern in this ecosystem: when top-tier forums enforce content restrictions, the infrastructure simply relocates. Despite the volatility of top-tier forums, DWIQ operatives, through direct 1:1 engagement with IABs, do not observe a material decline in access offers.
Platform Capabilities
Leak Bazaar’s announced capabilities describe a four-stage processing pipeline designed to refine raw exfiltrated data into marketable products:
Automated filtering: ML-assisted removal of system debris, such as OS backups, DLLs, and ISOs, combined with NLP-based text analysis of document arrays.
DBMS reverse engineering: Server-side parsing of raw database dumps from SQL, SAP, and Oracle exports into structured Excel and CSV outputs containing financial transactions, payroll records, and counterparty data. The actor described this feature as currently in beta.
Categorization: Processed data is segmented into buyer-oriented categories, including quarterly financial reports (QFRs), M&A data, R&D material, including source code, and personal data.
Manual validation: In-house analyst review of extracted data prior to listing.
The platform also claims the capability to generate structured analytical reports from processed data, identifying material such as potential OFAC/SDN sanctions violations, shadow accounting, and unpublished financial results. These reports are framed as negotiation pressure tools, replacing crude file listings with structured evidence of damaging material.
For buyers, the platform offers segmented purchasing. Rather than acquiring an entire raw dump, buyers can purchase specific data categories. Two purchasing models are available: exclusive, at full price with data removed after sale, and shared, at a lower price with data remaining available for resale. The revenue split is 70% to the data supplier and 30% to the platform. All transactions are conducted through Exploit’s escrow guarantor service.
Data submission requirements include exclusive and unpublished material, primarily English-language content, a minimum size of 100 GB, with preference for 1 TB or more, and targets with annual revenue exceeding $10 million. Priority sectors include technical development, biotechnology, pharmaceuticals, law, insurance, and finance. The platform’s stated exclusion list mirrors standard CIS-aligned operating norms: no data from Russia, CIS countries, Ukraine, BRICS nations, or Iran.
For organizations whose data has been exfiltrated, the existence of a platform like Leak Bazaar changes the calculus of a ransomware incident. If the platform functions as advertised, refusal to pay a ransom no longer eliminates the value of stolen data. Instead, exfiltrated material can be refined, segmented by category, and resold, potentially multiple times to different buyer profiles, over an extended period. The effort SnowTeam is investing in processing infrastructure, analyst validation, and structured analytical reports suggests an intent to extract greater value per breach. For organizations that have experienced a breach, this means the operational life of stolen data may extend well beyond the initial incident, and post-breach monitoring must account for structured resale on platforms like Leak Bazaar, not only raw data dumps on conventional leak sites. Access to the platform itself becomes an intelligence priority.
Assessment
DWIQ assesses that Leak Bazaar represents a meaningful evolution in the ransomware ecosystem. The platform introduces a model in which exfiltrated data is treated as a refinable asset rather than a binary extortion instrument.
It is worth noting what DWIQ can and cannot assess about the motivation behind this platform. Leak Bazaar has emerged at a time when ransom payment rates have declined to historic lows and the IAB market has experienced significant access price deflation. These conditions are consistent with an environment in which actors are seeking alternative revenue streams from exfiltrated data. However, SnowTeam has not directly confirmed to DWIQ operatives that declining payment rates motivated the creation of Leak Bazaar. The platform may reflect a strategic response to ecosystem economics, or it may reflect an independent entrepreneurial initiative. DWIQ has not elicited a definitive answer on this point and treats the correlation as a possible indicator rather than a confirmed causal link.
Regardless, the platform addresses a real and observable gap in the ransomware monetization chain. The transition from a single-group tool, as first disclosed to DWIQ operatives in October 2025, to a multi-supplier exchange with a referral program and active RaaS group presence on T1erOne suggests that SnowTeam is positioning Leak Bazaar as ecosystem infrastructure rather than an Akira-specific project.
DWIQ will continue to engage with SnowTeam to monitor the platform’s operational status and assess the authenticity of its technical claims.
Open-source coverage of Leak Bazaar appeared beginning March 25–27, 2026, from Flare, DarkWebInformer, CyberSecurityNews, and other outlets. Notably, the Chainalysis 2026 Crypto Crime Report cited DWIQ’s operational data on IAB access pricing in its analysis of the ransomware supply chain. Readers interested in the broader market context around initial access pricing and IAB activity as a leading indicator for ransomware payments may find DWIQ’s contribution to that report relevant.
Distributed Brute Force Cluster: Continued Industrialization of Offensive Tooling
BLOT: In late March 2026, through continued engagement with SnowTeam, DWIQ operatives were offered a fully productized distributed brute-forcing system for $50,000 USD, featuring automated scanning, credential validation, and brute-force capabilities across eight enterprise VPN and remote access protocols. The tool is consistent with the industrialization trend documented in DWIQ’s Q4 2025 Trend Report but represents a distinct product with additional technical specifications and a defined commercial offering. SnowTeam also advertised this tool on Exploit and other forums, from which the listing was subsequently removed.
Background
DWIQ’s Q4 2025 Trend Report documented the emergence of modular brute-forcing frameworks within the IAB ecosystem, including a distributed JAR-based system offered by an associate of Akira ransomware and the proliferation of white-labeled account-checking tools such as the “Canva” and “RedTeam” checkers. That report assessed the shift toward industrialized automation as a response to diminishing returns in traditional targeted exploitation, enabled in part by AI-assisted development.
The tool offered to DWIQ in Q1 2026 is not identical to those previously reported. It is a separate product, but it fits squarely within the same market and methodology.
Technical Specifications
The product was offered to DWIQ operatives in Russian, with the following key capabilities described:
Full-cycle automation: Integrated Masscan, written in C, for network scanning; a checker module with honeypot filtering and protocol identification; brute-force functionality with proxy rotation and intelligent load management; and a Python-based result-dumping module with Kerberos name extraction for credential re-enumeration.
Geo-isolation: Region-specific operation across the United States, Europe, and Asia using localized proxy pools and credential databases.
Dynamic scaling: Architecture designed to add capacity to individual pipeline stages, scan, check, and brute, without stopping the process.
Fault tolerance: Built-in monitoring and recovery; if a node fails, tasks are automatically returned to the queue.
Web-based control panel: Centralized interface for monitoring CPU load, requests per second, proxy status, and managing discovered access.
Supported protocols: Fortinet, Cisco, SonicWall, WatchGuard, GlobalProtect (Palo Alto), Sophos, RDWeb, and Citrix. This list maps closely to DWIQ’s observed access-type distribution throughout 2025, in which Fortinet VPN, SonicWall VPN, Cisco VPN, and RDWeb consistently accounted for the majority of access offers.
The package includes Masscan source code with build instructions and deployment scripts; a dumper module with source code and an Incus container for deployment; checker and brute-force services as unobfuscated JAR files, with source code available at additional cost; a user manual; lifetime technical support; and custom development at additional cost.
Price: $50,000 USD.
Assessment
DWIQ assesses that this offering confirms the continued maturation of the offensive tooling market within the IAB ecosystem. The $50,000 price point reflects enterprise-grade pricing for a turnkey product with deployment support, ongoing maintenance, and custom development options, a commercial model more consistent with legitimate software sales than with ad hoc underground tooling.
The protocol coverage list is operationally significant. Fortinet, SonicWall, and Cisco VPN products have consistently dominated DWIQ’s access-type observations since Q3 2025. The inclusion of all three in a single automated pipeline, alongside five additional remote access protocols, enables a single operator to conduct credential attacks across a target’s entire remote access surface from a unified interface.
This tool does not introduce a novel attack methodology. Brute-forcing remains the underlying technique. The advancement is architectural: the combination of Masscan-based discovery, automated honeypot filtering, proxy-rotated credential attacks, Kerberos-based re-enumeration, and centralized management constitutes a complete attack pipeline that reduces the operational overhead traditionally required to scale access brokerage. This directly enables the volume increases DWIQ has documented: tools like this lower the technical barrier for an individual to enter the IAB market and produce access offers at scale, contributing to the explosive growth DWIQ observed throughout 2025, from approximately 2,700 access offers in late 2024 to approximately 5,800 in Q3 2025. When turnkey offensive tooling is available for a one-time purchase, the number of actors capable of generating high-volume access offers increases, and the ransomware supply chain benefits from a deeper, cheaper pool of initial access.
Defensive Considerations
Enforce account lockout policies and rate limiting on all internet-facing VPN and remote access endpoints, particularly Fortinet SSL-VPN, SonicWall SSL-VPN, Cisco AnyConnect, and RDWeb gateways.
Monitor authentication logs for distributed, low-and-slow brute-force patterns: multiple failed attempts across different accounts from rotating source IPs, consistent with proxy-rotated automated tooling.
Audit all remote access infrastructure for default, test, service, or migrated accounts that lack MFA enforcement. These remain the primary credential targets for automated brute-force campaigns.
Restrict management interfaces to trusted IP ranges to reduce the externally reachable attack surface for scanning tools like Masscan.
