Why Third-Party Risk Demands a New Intelligence Standard

Third-party compromise is not new — but the scale and opacity of access paths are growing. Mass exploitation, infostealer logs, and poor hygiene in MSPs create invisible exposure that traditional tooling misses.

Most alerts focus on symptoms: malware detections, abnormal log-ins, phishing attempts, anomalous behavior. The real question is about root posture risk — why the access was possible in the first place: an overprivileged vendor account, an unmonitored VPN user, a password stolen weeks ago and never rotated.

How We Got Here

What we call "third-party risk" today is the byproduct of a decade of scaling that accelerated under pressure of convenience, cost savings, and reliance on third-party expertise — not intentional security design. The system was architected for efficiency, not resilience.

COVID-19 was the inflection point. In 2020, enterprises moved to remote access overnight — opening ports, extending privileges, and scaling trust faster than oversight. RDP attacks grew 768% in 2020, totaling 29 billion attempts (ReliaQuest). Ransomware attacks spiked 148% in March 2020 alone (VMware). Threat actors exploited urgency — not technical flaws — as their entry point.

By Q1 2025, ransomware reached record highs: 2,000+ victims, 70+ active groups (GuidePoint Security) — many exploiting remote services and credential access. Shared tools became shared exposure. Vendor ecosystems expanded faster than they could be secured.

Anatomy of Third-Party Exposure

Infostealer Logs → Exposed Credentials
Malware (RedLine, Raccoon, Vidar) steals browser-saved passwords, session cookies, and VPN/RMM credentials. Logs are circulated in private Telegram groups, access marketplaces, and via direct relationships with brokers.
Weak Segmentation → Pivot Path
If your HVAC vendor has access to your billing system, you've got a segmentation issue. Poor network isolation turns a vendor compromise into full lateral movement.
Overlapping Identities → Lateral Movement
Vendors reusing passwords across client environments. IT admins using personal email as backup for corp accounts. Single SSO shared between vendors and internal users = risk across tenants.
Unnoticed Reuse → Repeat Compromise
Credential stolen months ago via infostealer, password never rotated, new IAB picks it up and sells it again. Often reused across multiple SaaS platforms (O365, VPN, Salesforce).
